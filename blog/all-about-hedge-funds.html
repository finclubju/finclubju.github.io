All about Hedge Funds

Hedge funds are managed in many developed countries, many of which manage multi-billion dollar portfolios. Participation in hedge funds is often limited to small circles of wealthy investors, as they often require large initial investments.
Finance Blog Opinion

Hedge funds are investment vehicles for high net worth individuals, institutional investors and other accredited investors. The term "hedge" is used because historically these funds have focused on long-short equity strategies to hedge risk by buying and shorting assets at the same time. Actually, Hedge funds are actively managed investment funds whose managers employ a wide range of strategies, including buying with borrowed money and trading in esoteric assets, in an effort to outperform average investment returns for their clients. They are considered risky alternative investment options.

Insights of Hedge Funds

The term "Hedge Fund" helps tell a story. Traditional investment trust managers can use some of the assets available for hedged bets. This is a bet in the opposite direction of the fund's focus and is taken to offset the loss of core holdings.
For example, a fund manager focused on a well-performing circular sector in a booming economy, such as Travel, investing some of its assets in non-circular sector stocks such as food and energy companies. If the economy slows, non-circulating stock returns should offset the loss of circulating stocks.
Hedge fund managers are pushing this concept to the limit today. In fact, their funds have little to do with hedging, except for a few choices that adhere to the concept of the original hedge fund known as the classic long / short equity model.

Categories of Hedge Funds

Hedge funds can pursue a variety of strategies, including macro, equity, relative value, distressed securities, and activism.

  • Macroeconomic hedge funds invest in equities, bonds and currencies in the hope of benefiting from changes in macroeconomic variables such as global interest rates and national economic policies.
  • Equity Hedge Funds are global or country specific and allow you to invest in attractive equities while hedging the stock market downturn by shorting overvalued equities and stock indices.
  • Relative Value Hedge Funds exploit price or spread inefficiencies. Other hedge fund strategies include aggressive growth, revenue, emerging markets, value and short-term sales.

Benefits of Hedge Funds

  • Hedge funds donate large sums of money each year to non-profit organizations that benefit society. This includes everything from organizations that benefit the homeless, children, world hunger, arts and education.
  • An investment strategy that can generate positive returns in both up and down stock and bond markets.
  • Reduced overall portfolio risk and volatility in a balanced portfolio.
  • Increased revenue.

Risk Factor of Hedge Funds

Hedge funds may use riskier strategies in riskier ways. Notably, they often use leverage. This means they use borrowed money to buy more assets to multiply their potential returns (or losses). They also invest in derivatives such as options and futures.
In short, they are free to choose esoteric investments that conservative investors won't reach for.
In particular, hedge funds are not as strictly regulated by the Securities and Exchange Commission as mutual funds.
As might be expected, the appeal of many hedge funds lies in the reputation of their managers, many of whom are considered stars in the closed world of hedge fund investing.
These money managers are not cheap. Hedge funds generally charge a fee of 1-2% of assets plus a "performance fee" of around 20% of profits.

Comparison with Other Investment Options

Hedge funds, mutual funds, and exchange-traded funds (ETFs) are all pools of funds donated by many investors who seek to make money for themselves and their clients.
Hedge funds, like some mutual funds, have few ETFs, but by professional managers who buy and sell specific investments with the goal of surpassing market returns, specific sectors, or market indexes. It is actively managed.
Hedge funds strive for the highest possible returns and take the greatest risk to achieve them.
It is less regulated than competing products and gives you the flexibility to invest in almost any asset class available, including options, derivatives and esoteric investments that trusts cannot handle.
Another difference is cost. Hedge funds charge much higher fees than other investment options.

To conclude, wealthy individuals who can afford to diversify into hedge funds can be attracted to their manager's reputation, the specific assets in which the fund is invested, or the unique strategies it employs.
In some cases, the techniques used by hedge funds, such as combining leverage and complex derivative transactions, may not even be approved by regulators if pursued by mutual funds or other types of regulated investment vehicles.
