The Indian Economy - Bright Spot on a Dark Horizon

Recently the IMF forecasts the economic growth of countries for the year 2023. While the IMF is fearing an all-time high Recession, India’s GDP Growth has astoundingly peaked at 6.8 % in contrast to that of Developed countries which ranged from 1% to 3%.
Finance Blog Opinion

History

Let us take a look back at the History of the Indian Economy in the late 1900s. Unlike India, most of the colonized nations got their Economic Freedom before Political Freedom. India’s whole economy was completely crushed by the British Government. Not only there was political turmoil in the whole country but also India was going through its most difficult times in terms of economic growth. The ‘Central Planner Economic Model’ of Government stagnated economic growth. Not many reforms were made until 1991 when India’s foreign Reserves hit the rock bottom and India was pushed to secure a loan of $1.4 Bn from the IMF. Witnessing the horrible condition of India’s economy, the Govt. made some serious reforms that revolutionized the whole economy of India. In the Budget of 1991, Dr. Manmohan Singh introduced a completely new Economic Policy to India. The whole edifice of India’s economic reform was built on the desire to unleash the free spirit of Private Enterprises and Globalization. The Govt. of India gave full freedom to Private Enterprises and didn’t meddle much with their internal affairs and expansion. That step gave a phenomenal boost to our economy and the Budget of 1991 went down in history as The Economic Liberalisation of India. Now if we go by the GDP growth of India it’s clearly noticeable that after 2010 India’s economy boosted like nothing else reaching new peaks, and breaking records every year.

Factors Responsible for Economic Boom

1. NPS :

The implementation of NPS – National Pension Scheme by our former PM Mr. Atal Bihari Vajpayee. During the early 2000s, a shocking report was released. In that report, it was mentioned that although civil servants account for only a tiny fraction of the population still their pension accounts for about 60 percent of India’s GDP. It was an absolute burden to the economy of the country. This is when the Vajpayee Govt. came up with a plan known as the National Pension Scheme. Under this scheme the govt. of India will roll out a pension to civil servants according to the market condition of India. Unlike the old pension scheme the Govt. invests a portion of the employee’s salary into Government Bonds, Corporate Bonds, and Equities, and the employees will receive pensions according to the returns that will be generated by these financial instruments. In 2009 this scheme was rolled out to every citizen of India. So huge capital was pumped into this fund which was being used by the Govt. of India and Private Enterprises to establish themselves and generate huge profits contributing to the overall growth of India’s Economy. Moreover, under the NPS system, the Govt. of India is not directly responsible for the pension of the people. Both the Market as well as the scheme went hand in hand and generated huge returns to the investors or the pensioners and also benefitted the whole economy of India.

2. Influx of FDI- Direct Foreign Investment:

FDI in simple words means Investment made by Foreign Investors. In the FY 2014-15 India’s FDI inflow shot up nearly 45% (YoY Growth) reaching $45.5 Bn Dollars which again increased to $60.22 Bn and further to the highest ever FDI inflow of about $83.57 Bn reported during FY 2021-22. There can be many reasons for the upward trajectory of FDIs like the credible success of the Indian Diaspora, strong and reliable Govt. flexibility in setting up units of companies, and so on. In a nutshell, a huge inflow of FDIs resulted in the economic boom of India.

3. Growth of the IT Service Sector:

The Immense growth of the IT service sector in India pushed the economy to reach new heights. The IT-BPM (Information technology and Business Process Management) sector of India has a share of 9.3% of the country’s GDP, making it one of the biggest sectors to contribute to India’s economic growth. The IT sector captures 56% of the global outsourcing market. The Software Technology Park of India (STPI) reported that IT companies associated with it made software exports worth $16.29 billion in only the first quarter of the financial year 2022. 4.5 lakh employees were hired till February 2022 in the first quarter of 2022. This has been the highest-ever addition in any given year. There is significant participation of women as out of the total new employees, 44% were women. The Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) inflows in the IT sector (computer hardware and software, to be precise) between April 2000-December 2021 stood at $81.31 billion (14.19% of the FDI inflows). The data from the Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT) proves that it was the second-largest sector to have received such high FDI inflows

4. Atmanirbhar Bharat:

Atmanirbhar means 'self-reliant'. The main objective of this scheme is to shape India as an Export Oriented Country. Increasing export is crucial to fill up the Fiscal Deficit and the profits generated by exports can ultimately be utilized for strengthening the infrastructure or the military of India. This scheme endorsed India to make records in Defence Exports worth Rs. 13000 Crore in FY 2021-22 and now gearing up for world domination.

5. Huge Population:

The huge population of India contributed to the huge workforce required by different sectors of India when utilized efficiently.

Conclusion

I enumerated some of the primary reasons for India’s economic growth. India’s diplomatic stance on Russia-Ukraine War enabled us to buy Oil at the cheapest price from Russia. The diversified industries of India are a strong reason for India never going in the Sri-Lankan way. Apart from these, there are several other reasons for the present growth of India. India’s economic growth has been not only lauded by the developing countries but also by Kristalina Georgieva, MD of IMF.
